  <h1>GST API vs Manual Lookup — Why Hardcoding Rates Is a Compliance Time Bomb</h1>

  <p class="article-deck">Three approaches to GST rate lookup in your Indian application. Two of them will give you a compliance notice within 18 months. Here is the honest breakdown — no sales pitch, just what actually happens to each approach in production.</p>

  <h2>The three approaches every Indian developer uses</h2>

  <p><strong>Option A: Hardcode a rate table.</strong> You copy the GST rates from a government PDF into a JSON file, a database table, or a <code>switch</code> statement. Ship it. Zero ongoing cost.</p>

  <p><strong>Option B: Scrape the GST portal.</strong> You write a scraper that hits <code>services.gst.gov.in</code> and extracts rates on demand. Feels clever. Feels like you built something.</p>

  <p><strong>Option C: Use a GST API.</strong> You call an endpoint that returns the correct rate in JSON. Pay a monthly fee. Move on.</p>

  <p>Most developers start with Option A. Here is what happens 6 months later.</p>

  <h2>What goes wrong with hardcoded rates</h2>

  <p>The GST Council meets roughly every 2–3 months. Each meeting produces notifications that change rates across dozens of product categories. In September 2025, the Council restructured the entire rate schedule — eliminating the 12% slab for most goods and moving them to either 5% or 18%, and introducing a new 40% slab for luxury and demerit goods.</p>

  <p>Every developer who hardcoded rates before September 2025 was producing incorrect invoices the morning after that notification went live.</p>

  <div class="callout">
    <strong>The penalty for incorrect GST invoices:</strong> 10% of the tax amount as penalty, plus interest at 18% per annum from the date the tax was due. The CBIC issued over 93,000 notices for incorrect HSN declarations in FY2024 alone. The notices do not care that you hardcoded the rates in good faith.
  </div>

  <p>The second problem with hardcoded rates is conditions. India's GST structure is not flat. The same HSN code can attract different rates depending on whether the product is branded or unbranded, pre-packaged or loose, supplied B2B or B2C, intrastate or interstate, below or above a price threshold. A hardcoded table cannot resolve conditions. It returns one number. That number is wrong for a significant percentage of your transactions.</p>

  <p>For example: unbranded basmati rice (HSN 10063012) is taxed at 0%. Branded, pre-packaged basmati rice is taxed at 5%. If your hardcoded table says 5% for all basmati rice, you are over-collecting GST from customers buying unbranded stock — and under-collecting for the reverse. Both create liabilities.</p>

  <h2>What goes wrong with portal scraping</h2>

  <p>Scraping <code>services.gst.gov.in</code> seems like the smart middle ground — you are reading live government data, so it is always current. Here is what actually happens:</p>

  <p><strong>GSTN blocks aggressive scrapers.</strong> The portal has rate limiting and IP blocking. Any application doing more than a handful of lookups per minute will get blocked. In production, when your checkout process calls the scraper, it times out, your customer sees an error, and you lose the sale.</p>

  <p><strong>The portal structure changes without notice.</strong> When the government redesigned the portal in 2024, dozens of scraper-based applications broke overnight. No changelog, no API versioning, no migration guide. Your regex patterns stop matching and you have no fallback.</p>

  <p><strong>Response times are 3–15 seconds.</strong> A government portal is not optimised for API throughput. Your checkout flow that should complete in under 2 seconds now takes 17 seconds because it is waiting for a government website to respond.</p>

  <p><strong>Rate change lag.</strong> GST Council notifications are published and implemented at the same time. Your portal scraper reads whatever the portal currently shows — but government portals sometimes take 24–72 hours to reflect new rates after a council notification. During that window, you are still scraping stale data.</p>

  <h2>What a production GST API gives you</h2>

  <p>A GST API built for production — not a hobbyist project — solves all of the above. Here is what to look for and what to verify before you integrate.</p>

  <p><strong>Always-current data.</strong> The API should update within 24 hours of any CBIC notification. Not "when we get around to it." Within 24 hours, with a documented update policy and a webhook to notify you when rates change.</p>

  <p><strong>Condition resolution.</strong> The rate returned should not be a flat number. It should be the rate that applies to <em>your specific supply</em> — with the condition evaluated against your parameters. See the <a href="https://gstaccelerator.in/blog/cgst-sgst-igst-explained">CGST/SGST/IGST explainer</a> for how supply type alone changes which tax applies.</p>

  <p><strong>Notification reference in every response.</strong> Every rate returned should include the exact CBIC notification that authorises it. When your CA questions a rate six months later, you need to show them: Notification No. 09/2025-CT(Rate), Schedule II. Without that reference, you have a number with no legal backing.</p>

  <p><strong>Under 200ms response time with a guaranteed SLA.</strong> Not "usually fast." A documented 99.5% uptime guarantee with a response time commitment.</p>

  <h1>CGST, SGST and IGST Explained — How to Split GST Correctly for Every Transaction</h1>

  <p class="article-deck">India's GST is three separate taxes — not one. Which one applies depends entirely on where your buyer is. Get it wrong and your GSTR-1 misfires, your buyer loses ITC, and the wrong government collects the money. Here is the complete, plain-English breakdown with rupee examples.</p>

  <h2>The rule — in one sentence</h2>

  <p><strong>Intrastate supply</strong> (seller and buyer in the same state): CGST + SGST apply. Equal halves of the total rate.</p>

  <p><strong>Interstate supply</strong> (seller and buyer in different states): IGST applies. Full rate to the Centre, which settles with the destination state.</p>

  <p>That is the entire decision tree. Everything else is application.</p>

  <div class="callout">
    <strong>The identity to memorise:</strong> CGST = SGST always. IGST = CGST + SGST always. If the total GST rate is 18%, then intrastate means CGST 9% + SGST 9%. Interstate means IGST 18%. The rupee amount collected from the customer is identical. What changes is which government receives it.
  </div>

  <h2>Worked examples in rupees</h2>

  <h3>Example 1: Laptop sold intrastate (seller in Maharashtra, buyer in Maharashtra)</h3>

  <div class="table-wrap">

  <p>The CGST ₹7,200 goes to the Central Government. The SGST ₹7,200 stays with Maharashtra. Both appear as separate line items on the invoice. Both go into your GSTR-1 as separate entries.</p>

  <h2>The complete supply-type decision tree</h2>

  <div class="table-wrap">
    <table>
      <thead><tr><th>Situation</th><th>Tax applicable</th><th>Who receives it</th></tr></thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr><td>Seller state = Buyer state</td><td>CGST + SGST</td><td>Centre + Seller's state</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Seller state ≠ Buyer state</td><td>IGST</td><td>Centre (settles with destination state)</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Supply to a Union Territory</td><td>CGST + UTGST</td><td>Centre + Union Territory</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Export or SEZ supply</td><td>IGST @ 0%</td><td>Zero-rated — refund available</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Import of goods</td><td>IGST + Customs</td><td>Centre</td></tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </div>

  <div class="callout-blue">
    <strong>Union Territory note:</strong> Delhi, Chandigarh, Puducherry, Ladakh, Dadra and Nagar Haveli, Daman and Diu, and Lakshadweep are Union Territories. Supplies within these areas use CGST + UTGST — not CGST + SGST. The rate is identical. The receiving authority is the Union Territory administration, not a state government.
  </div>

  <h2>The five most common mistakes</h2>

  <p><strong>1. Applying IGST on intrastate sales.</strong> The most common error in e-commerce platforms that don't check the buyer's state before tax calculation. The total tax is the same but the tax filing is wrong — IGST collected on an intrastate sale shows as a mismatch in GSTR-2B reconciliation.</p>

  <p><strong>2. Treating SGST and IGST as alternatives.</strong> They are never alternatives for the same transaction. Either CGST+SGST applies, or IGST applies. Never both, never a choice.</p>

  <p><strong>3. Assuming Union Territory = State.</strong> UT supplies use UTGST, not SGST. Billing software that auto-fills SGST for all domestic intrastate supplies will produce incorrect invoices for buyers in Delhi, Chandigarh, and other UTs.</p>

  <p><strong>4. Hardcoding the supply type.</strong> An e-commerce platform where sellers are spread across states cannot hardcode "intrastate" or "interstate" — it depends on the seller's registered state vs the buyer's delivery state for each individual order. See <a href="https://gstaccelerator.in/blog/gst-api-vs-manual-lookup">why hardcoding GST data creates compliance risk</a>.</p>

  <p><strong>5. Not separating CGST and SGST on the invoice.</strong> The GST invoice format mandated by CBIC requires CGST and SGST to appear as separate line items. A single "GST 18%" line without the split is a non-compliant invoice.</p>

  <h2>How developers handle this automatically</h2>

  <p>Every transaction needs to know the supply type before the tax split can be calculated. The correct approach is to determine supply type at order time — compare the seller's GSTIN state code against the buyer's delivery state — and then pass that to the rate API.</p>

  <p>Here is the complete pattern in Python using the <a href="https://gstaccelerator.in/docs">GST Accelerator API</a>:</p>

<pre><span class="cm"># pip install gstaccelerator</span>
